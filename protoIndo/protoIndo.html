Proto-Indo-European: An Introduction

For countless centuries, people have been noticing particular similarities between seemingly unrelated languages. For example, the words for father, in the languages English, Latin, Greek, Spanish, German, respectively, are: father, pater, pat√©ras, padre, vater. And there were loads of these kinds of parallels between languages, for many words.

For a long time these patterns were waved off as simple word-borrowing from languages spoken in close proximity. But in the 1500's, new trade routes with India exposed Europeans to a new language: Sanskrit, whose word for father was piter. This was shocking, since relations with India had not been going on nearly long enough for such word-borrowing. And so the idea of Proto-Indo-European was proposed.

Scholars decided there must be a sort of parent language, which they called Proto-Indo-European or PIE, spoken roughly 6,000 years ago. As speakers migrated across the continent, their dialects' pronounciations, morphology, and vocabulary would have, over time, slowly diverged into most of the languages now spoken between Europe and India.

These languages are called Indo-European languages, which together compose the Indo-European language family.

Linguistic Reconstruction

No direct evidence exists of Proto-Indo-European, so linguists resort to a method known as linguistic reconstruction: comparing the vocabulary and historical sound changes of known languages to deduce, or "reconstruct", the original PIE language.
